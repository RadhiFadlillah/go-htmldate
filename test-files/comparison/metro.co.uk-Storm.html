Storm Ciarán may fire ‘sting jet’ at UK causing 30 miles of devastation

As Storm Ciarán continues to batter the south coast of England and the Channel Isles, meteorologists are warning that the worst could be yet to come in the form of a ‘sting jet’.

The storm has already been labelled a ‘weather bomb’ due to its explosive power, caused by a rapid drop in pressure.

In a similar phenomenon, a sting jet occurs when part of the jet stream descends to Earth’s surface very quickly, gaining momentum as it goes and creating a small core of even more extreme winds.

Bob Henson, a meteorologist and science writer with Yale Climate Connections, said this ‘punches’ a small area of the surface with very high winds, he said, causing serious damage.

The name refers to the shape the cloud takes as it wraps around the centre of an area of low pressure – similar to a scorpion’s curled tail.

If a sting jet does arrive, it could be amongst the handful of strongest storms in the region for the last 200 years, said Mr Henson. 

‘Be prepared for things to be flying around,’ he warned.

If the sting jet does hit, it will most likely affect only a narrow area, perhaps 30 miles across, and last only a few hours.

But the Met Office states: ‘Regardless, with gusts of wind exceeding 100 mph there is clearly still the risk of damage.’

The UK was hit with a sting jet during the great storm of 1987, in which 22 people died.

Yesterday, the Met Office warned that climate change is making the UK wetter, as warmer air holds more moisture – around 7% for each degree.

Four quick facts about sting jets

  • The ‘sting in the tail’ is produced by a distinct jet of air – the sting jet
  • It starts out three or four kilometres above the ground and descends over three or four hours
  • Snow and rain falling into it evaporates and cools it as it descends, helping to accelerate it to high speeds
  • It can accelerate to more than 100 mph

Source: Met Office

Storm Ciarán is expected to dump vast quantities of rain across the south of the country.

‘There are a lot of attribution studies and other lines of evidence showing that autumn/winter storms like this are more damaging because of climate change,’ said Dr Friederike Otto, of the Grantham Institute at Imperial College London.

‘That’s because the rainfall associated with these types of storms is more severe due to climate change, and the storm surges are higher and thus more damaging due to the higher sea levels.’

Dr Melissa Lazenby, of the University of Sussex, said the UK will be likely to see more winter storms as the climate warms, which would increase the risk of flooding and storm surges.

‘The Met Office states that increased winter storms, including disproportionately more severe storms, are projected to cross the UK in the future, with fewer to the north, over Iceland, and fewer to the south, over central and southern Europe,’ she said.

‘Therefore, research indicates that the UK should expect to see storms more frequently than it is seeing today and such additional adaptation and mitigation measures will be necessary to mitigate their impacts such as flooding and storm surges.’
